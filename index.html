Main Contents

The Treaty of Waitangi is New Zealand’s founding document, signed on 6 February 1840 at Waitangi in the Bay of Islands. It was an agreement in Māori and English between the British Crown and about 540 Māori chiefs, aiming to protect Māori rights and was the basis for the British annexation of New Zealand12. Today, it remains significant as it allows Māori to bring claims against the Crown for actions that may have disadvantaged them since 1840

Sidebar

The Treaty of Waitangi is New Zealand’s founding document. It takes its name from the place in the Bay of Islands where it was first signed, on 6 February 1840. This day is now a public holiday in New Zealand. The Treaty is an agreement, in Māori and English, that was made between the British Crown and about 540 Māori rangatira (chiefs).
